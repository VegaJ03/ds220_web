About Dataset¶

The Billboard Hot 100 is the music industry standard record chart in the United States for songs, published weekly by Billboard magazine. Chart rankings are based on sales, radio play, and online streaming in the United States. Every week, Billboard releases "The Hot 100" chart of songs that were trending on sales and airplay for that week. This dataset is a collection of all "The Hot 100" charts released since its inception in 1958.

Variables:

  1. date: date when the Top 100 ranking was released
  2. rank: ranking of the song during that specific week of Top 100 songs
  3. song: title of song
  4. artist: name of the artist who released the song
  5. last-week: last week song's ranking
  6. peak-rank: the song's peak ranking in Top 100
  7. weeks-on-board: total weeks the song stay in the ranking

Queries¶

  1. What is the relationship between last week rank and rank?

To explore this question, we took a sample size of 8000 out of 330086 songs from the dataset and create a scatter plot to check the relationship between last week rank vs rank:

In [29]:
# Relationship between 'last week rank' and 'rank'
sample_size = 8000
df_sample = df.sample(n=sample_size)  

# Scatter plot for the relationship between 'last week rank' and 'rank'
plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))
plt.scatter(df_sample['last-week'], df_sample['rank'], alpha=0.5)
plt.title(f'Scatter Plot: Last Week Rank vs. Rank (Random Sample of {sample_size} rows)')
plt.xlabel('Last Week Rank')
plt.ylabel('Rank')
plt.show()

The graph shows positive correlation between last week rank vs rank. It suggests that there is a tendency for higher values of 'last week rank' to be associated with higher values of 'rank,' and vice versa.

It could also mean that songs that were ranked high in the previous week tend to continue being ranked high in the current week. Conversely, songs with lower rankings in the previous week tend to maintain lower rankings.
